01 The Agentic Firm Operating Model

From adoption to strategy — the practice as a four-layer agent stack wrapped by a human control plane

Adoption is every staffer running their own agent in their own context. Strategy is one governed bench the whole practice runs on, wrapped by a human control plane of licensed principals. This chapter draws the firm as four layers — deterministic engines, specialist agents, orchestrators, and the human plane — and shows where Book 1 left off and this book takes over.

Adoption vs strategy — why the firm is not just everyone using agents

Book 1, The Agentic Accountant, followed the individual practitioner. One desk, one agent, one person carrying the verify burden. That is the adoption picture, and it is where most firms stop. Every staffer gets a licence, every staffer runs their own agent in their own chat window, every staffer decides their own prompts and their own tolerances. Productivity goes up. The liability surface goes up with it, and nobody notices until a draft leaves the building un-reviewed.

Strategy is different. Strategy is one governed agent stack the whole practice runs on, with shared parameters, shared gates, shared audit trail, and a licensed human plane wrapped around it. The agent, shared-services, and tooling stack is identical whether you are solo or a firm of fifty. What changes is the human organisation — how credentials distribute, how duties segregate, how deep the sign-off chain runs. That difference is the entire reason this book exists.

Consider the same task handled both ways. A staffer asks the agent to compute the super guarantee on a grossed-up bonus.

Strategy. The staffer invokes /master-orchestrator on the firm bench. The run loads the client profile from /client-context/ABC001/, reads SG 12% from the shared current-year parameter table, delegates to payroll-agent, cites Super Guarantee (Administration) Act, stamps as-at June 2026, and writes every hop to the audit trail. A different licensed human signs the pay event.

Adoption. The staffer runs their own agent on their own laptop with their own prompt. The model reasons about SG from memory, returns a figure, and the staffer approves it themselves because there is no one else in the loop. Same model. Different liability surface, no trail, no segregation.

Same model. Same arithmetic. The first run is defensible to the ATO. The second run is a story a principal tells a court. The difference is governance, and governance only exists at firm scale.

The punchline is simple. Adoption buys productivity. Strategy buys defensibility, and a regulated practice only survives on defensibility.

My take: most firms I see are stuck at adoption and calling it transformation. They have forty seats running forty private agent histories. That is not a firm, it is a shared office. The day a regulator asks who approved a lodged figure and the answer is "the model did," the strategy conversation finally happens. Have it now, not after the incident.

The four-layer firm stack — engines, agents, orchestrators, the human control plane

The agentic firm is four layers wrapped by a fifth. From the bottom up: deterministic engines that compute every regulated number, specialist agents that route and draft, orchestrators that sequence the work, and tooling and MCP servers that reach the systems of record. The fifth layer, wrapped around the whole stack, is the human control plane of licensed principals, managers, and reviewers. Every layer has a job. None of them does the job of another.

The engines are not a separate box on the diagram. They live inside the agents and inside the systems of record. Xero's double-entry ledger is an engine. The ATO tax tables behind payroll-agent are an engine. The cleared-balance arithmetic inside bank-reconciliation-agent is an engine. The firm's discipline is that every regulated number flows from one of these engines and never from an LLM's prose. That is the determinism-first principle, and chapter 02 unpacks it at firm scale.

A request enters at L1 and flows down through L2 to L4 and back. master-orchestrator parses the request, classifies the domain, and routes to the right specialist. The specialist calls the engine, drafts the artefact, and writes its output to the workpaper store at L3. The audit-trail-agent seals every hop. The output rises back up to L1, where the human-gate-coordinator holds it for the named gate. Nothing leaves the firm until a licensed human at L0 signs.

# The request lifecycle, end to end
Intake
  -> Acceptance gate (independence + conflict check)
  -> Routing (master-orchestrator classifies domain)
  -> Evidence (every output written to workpaper envelope, hash-sealed)
  -> Human gate (reserved-act boundary: H1 / H2 / H3)
  -> Sign-off and seal (registered human approves, lodgement fires)

Notice what the lifecycle does not say. It does not say "the agent decides." The agent routes, drafts, and stops. The lifecycle is a chain of named hand-offs, each one recorded, each one separable from the next. That chain is what makes a firm run different from a solo run with better tools.

The human control plane at firm scale — partners, managers, reviewers, the bench

The control plane is the humans wrapped around the agent stack. It has three roles: partners and directors who carry non-delegable sign-off, registered professionals who carry the regulated acts, and qualified reviewers who carry the practitioner review. The plane is what turns a bench of agents into a governed bench. Without it, the agents are just fast drafters with no accountability.

The control plane is formal, not advisory. A gate is a durable, resumable interrupt node. The output is held. A qualified reviewer is notified with an evidence pack. The reviewer records approve, reject, or rework with identity and timestamp into the immutable trail. Any attempt to skip the gate is itself a CRITICAL incident. The plane never moves out of the way for convenience.

Firm scale adds something the solo principal cannot have: tiered sign-off. The solo practitioner has one chokepoint — themselves. The firm has a chain: preparer, reviewer or manager, engagement partner, and for audit work an independent engagement-quality reviewer under ASA 220 and ASQM 2. That chain is the firm's structural advantage, and it is why some work is structurally barred from the solo build. A corporate audit opinion requires a registered company auditor plus an independent EQR who is not the engagement partner. No EQR, no opinion. The agent stack is identical. The human organisation is not.

How a firm run flows — master-orchestrator and practice-intelligence-orchestrator

The firm has two ends, and naming them is how you read the operating model. master-orchestrator is the outward flow. It routes client work. practice-intelligence-orchestrator is the inward flow. It reads the practice itself. Between them sits the bench — the team of seats running the same governed agents.

The outward flow starts when a request lands. master-orchestrator loads the client profile, classifies the domain from its keyword-to-route table, and delegates to a domain orchestrator or a specialist via the Task tool. The child runs, the engine computes, the audit trail seals, and the response returns with a compliance status marker. Here is the real routing table from the agent file.

Every routing target must exist as a file on disk. Every domain must have a named child. No orphan keywords. That table is the front door of the firm, and reading it is the first thing a principal does before trusting the orchestrator with a figure a client might rely on.

The inward flow never touches client work. practice-intelligence-orchestrator coordinates performance-analytics-agent, capacity-planning-agent, staff-utilization-agent, and client-success-agent. It reads XPM timesheet and WIP data and turns it into the strategic picture the principal decides on. It is the firm looking at itself.

# practice-intelligence-orchestrator delegating inward
performance = await Task(
    subagent_type="performance-analytics-agent",
    description="Generate practice performance insights",
    prompt="Read XPM WIP, utilisation, and realisation for the week; "
           "flag clients trending under margin; do not touch client books."
)

Hold the two ends in your head together. One routes work out into the client portfolio. One reads the firm's own numbers back. The principal sits between them, deciding both what the bench does next quarter and what the practice becomes next year.

My take: the firms that get the most out of this stack use the inward orchestrator as hard as the outward one. Most principals I meet can quote their top five clients by revenue but cannot quote their bottom five by realisation. The inward flow fixes that blind spot permanently, and it costs nothing because the XPM data already exists.

What the principal owns and what never gets delegated

The principal owns three things, and the agent never touches any of them. First, the reserved acts: a registered agent signs the BAS and the lodgement, a registered company auditor signs the audit opinion, an AFSL adviser signs the statement of advice, an external solicitor renders legal opinion. The agent prepares and stops. The human performs.

Second, the non-delegable judgements. Advice-scope classification. Going concern. The significance of an independence threat. These are partner-owned calls that no agent makes, because a model cannot hold a licence, cannot carry professional indemnity insurance, and cannot be held to account when its judgement fails.

Third, the constitution. The firm's shared brain — CLAUDE.md, AGENTS.md, DESIGN.md — is owned, reviewed, and re-baselined by the principal. The current-year parameter table lives there. The gate policy lives there. The agent directory lives there. Chapter 04 is the full treatment. For now, the point is that the principal owns the rules the whole bench runs on.

The escalation criteria inside master-orchestrator encode the first two. These are hard tripwires the orchestrator will not resolve in-agent.

# Always escalate to a licensed human when:
# - fraud or serious misconduct is suspected
# - a tax amount in dispute exceeds $100,000
# - legal proceedings are involved
# - the issue is complex and multi-jurisdictional
# - professional-conduct concerns arise

Those tripwires are the agent admitting its own boundary. When one fires, the orchestrator refuses to resolve the request itself, routes to a registered human, and flags the matter for the principal. That refusal is a feature. An agent that quietly handled a fraud suspicion would be a liability.

02 Determinism-First at Firm Scale

Engines own every regulated number — and segregation of duties stops any one agent or person from both computing and approving a figure

A deterministic engine computes every regulated figure. The agents only route, draft, organise, and chase. At firm scale that split becomes a runtime invariant, enforced by segregation of duties: the agent or person who computes a figure never approves it. This chapter shows the engine and agent boundary across three regulated domains and the control that makes the whole bench defensible.

The determinism-first principle, restated for a team

Book 1 stated the principle for one practitioner: your engine computes the figure, your agent routes and drafts, you carry the verify burden. At firm scale the principle hardens from a habit into an invariant. Every regulated figure — the GST fraction of 1/11, the PAYG withholding from the tax tables, the SG percentage, the cleared-balance arithmetic, the payroll-tax apportionment — is computed by a deterministic engine whose output is identical for identical inputs. The LLM agents route, draft, organise, chase, and flag. They compute nothing regulated. They decide nothing regulated.

The reason is liability plus reproducibility. A regulator or a court can re-derive a deterministic figure. An LLM's arithmetic cannot be defended that way. A model that "calculates the GST" produces a number you must re-check every time. An engine that computes the GST produces a number you audit once — by confirming the engine, the rate, and the inputs — and then trust for every transaction it processes. That audit-once property is what makes a firm possible. You cannot scale forty seats on re-check-every-time.

Engine-owned. The SG on a grossed-up bonus is computed by payroll-agent's engine: SG 12% for FY 2025-26 times the ordinary-time earnings, contribution base capped at $62,500 per quarter for FY 2025-26 (as at June 2026, per the ATO; becomes an annual $270,830 from 1 July 2026 under Payday Super). The agent reports the figure verbatim with its citation and stamps the workpaper.

Agent-owned. The model reasons about SG from memory and returns a figure. You re-check every pay run, forever, because a confident wrong answer reads identically to a confident right one.

The firm invariant is blunt. If a figure did not come from an engine, it came from a model. A model is the part that reasons. It is not the part that counts. The rest of this chapter is the mechanics of making that invariant real across a team.

The engine and agent boundary — who computes, who drafts

The boundary is crisp and it is drawn three ways here so you see the same split in three regulated domains. The general rule is that the autonomy tag binds the work product, not the agent. A single agent straddles levels across its lifecycle — a BAS path runs coding as A, draft as H3, lodge as H2 — and each output is tagged and gated separately.

Bank reconciliation — bank-reconciliation-agent. The cleared-balance arithmetic and the match-suggestion scoring are engine outputs. The agent drafts the exception note and routes low-confidence items. The confidence tiers themselves are engine-owned thresholds, not LLM moods. A match scored 96% is auto-applied. One scored 88% is routed assisted. One scored 72% is routed to human review. The engine decides the tier. The model writes the suggested coding.

# Confidence tiers as engine-owned routing (from bank-reconciliation-agent)
High (>95%):   autonomous transaction matching and reconciliation
Medium (80-95%): assisted processing with recommendations
Low (<80%):    human review required with detailed analysis

Escalation:
- variance > $5,000: require review
- suspicious patterns: fraud investigation
- accuracy degradation (<90%): process review

GST and BAS — bas-agent. The 10% GST fraction, the input-tax-credit apportionment, and the cash-versus-accrual totals are engine outputs. The agent drafts the BAS working paper and flags variance beyond tolerance. The firm's GST variance tolerance is plus or minus $2.00, set in the current-year parameter table. The drafting is an H3-class act. The lodgement is an H2-class act. The agent prepares and stops at the gate.

# The engine/agent boundary in one BAS flow
# compute GST fraction (1/11)       -> engine   (A)
# apportion input tax credits       -> engine   (A)
# draft BAS + working paper          -> LLM       (H3, registered-agent review)
# lodge the BAS post-gate            -> connector (H2, reserved act R2/R4)

Payroll — payroll-agent. PAYG withholding from the tax scales, SG percentage, leave loading, overtime rates, and payroll-tax thresholds and rates are all engine and parameter outputs. The agent drafts the pay event and routes exceptions. The agent's own halt rule is determinism-first enforced inside the agent definition: an STP validation failure halts the run, full stop.

# payroll-agent's determinism-first halt rule
ESCALATION RULES:
- STP validation failure:    halt payroll processing (critical)
- super below minimum (12%): manual review required
- award compliance breach:   escalate to HR

Notice the parenthetical in that halt rule. The agent file in the companion repo still prints "SG 11%" because the table was written for 2023-24 and never re-baselined. As at June 2026 the rate is 12%. That staleness is exactly the reason the current-year parameter table is owned and re-baselined every 1 July. Section 02.4 makes it the firm's single source of truth. Chapter 04 makes the re-baseline a one-edit operation.

Segregation of duties — the firm's oldest control, now agent-enforced

Segregation of duties is the firm's load-bearing control, and it is only fully possible at firm scale. The solo practitioner is structurally limited — the same person computes and reviews every figure, mitigated only by an external second reviewer. The firm has the chain: the agent or person who computes a figure is never the agent or person who approves it. For audit work, ASA 220 and ASQM 2 add an independent engagement-quality reviewer who is not the engagement partner. Three distinct humans plus the agent, for the judgements that need it.

In the agent stack, segregation becomes a runtime guarantee rather than a policy memo. compliance-agent carries the A/R/O/P classification and the four no-silent-bypass invariants: tag-binding, hold-on-R, sign-off-on-O, and gate-attempt logging. The human-gate-coordinator enforces an RBAC approver matrix. An over-qualified reviewer is fine — a registered tax agent approving a routine bank rec is excessive but not barred. An under-qualified reviewer is rejected — a BAS agent approving an audit opinion is blocked outright.

# Segregation enforced at the audit-trail event layer
{ actor: {type:"agent", id:"bas-agent", role:"tax"},
  verb:  "DRAFT", object: wp-0421, autonomy_tag:"R", reserved_acts:["R2"] }
# ... later, a DIFFERENT actor ...
{ actor: {type:"human", id:"sam", role:"registered-tax-agent",
           licence_class:"TASA"},
  verb:  "APPROVE", object: wp-0421, auth_method:"mfa" }
{ actor: {type:"agent", id:"ato-connector-agent"},
  verb:  "LODGE", object: wp-0421, autonomy_tag:"O", reserved_acts:["R4"] }

Three events, three distinct actor ids. The firm can prove to a regulator that compute is not approve is not lodge. That proof is the difference between an agent-assisted practice and an agent-run practice. The agents assist. The licensed humans carry the acts, and the audit trail records the separation.

The current-year parameter table — one source of truth, version-controlled

Every engine reads its rates from one version-controlled current-year parameter table. Where the LLM is allowed to act at firm scale

The LLM is allowed to do a lot. Most of the volume in a practice is matching, coding, drafting, and organising, and a model does that work well when a clear rule is being applied. The test for which band an act belongs in is simple. If the act applies a clear rule, it is A. If it interprets a tax law, it is H3 or higher. # The 4-gate decision procedure compliance-agent runs

elif act_in(reserved_acts):            return HUMAN_ONLY   # H1 or H2
elif needs_licence or third_party_reliance
     or expert_judgement or feeds_O:   return HUMAN_REVIEW # H3
else:                                   return AUTONOMOUS  # logged

The model does not negotiate a PROHIBITED into an AUTONOMOUS. It states the finding, names the gate, and stops.

Rollout and parity — keeping every seat on the same bench

The firm's parity discipline has four steps. First, the constitution is version-controlled and every seat pulls the same commit. Second, a pre-flight checks each seat can reach Xero, XPM, and the ATO channel before any figure is relied on. Third, a parity test runs the same task in all three runtimes and asserts the engine output is byte-identical — the LLM prose may vary, the GST total must not. Fourth, the audit trail records which runtime produced each workpaper, so the firm can prove consistency.

# same task, three runtimes — engine output must match
gst_claude   = run_in("claude-code", "compute Q3 GST", client="ABC001").gst
gst_opencode = run_in("opencode",     "compute Q3 GST", client="ABC001").gst
gst_codex    = run_in("codex",        "compute Q3 GST", client="ABC001").gst
assert gst_claude == gst_opencode == gst_codex   # engine-owned figure
# the working-paper PROSE may differ; the FIGURE must not

The parity test is the firm's evidence that the figure is host-independent. The first time a principal runs it and the three numbers agree to the cent, the operating model changes. The question stops being "did the model get the GST right" and becomes "is the right engine wired in." That is a smaller, faster, more defensible question.

Token expiry is the operational reality every seat hits. Xero access tokens are short-lived — roughly 30 minutes — with refresh tokens around 60 days. The exact lifetimes and the June 2026 scope set should be verified against developer.xero.com before print. xero-integration-specialist runs the refresh flow using the stored refresh token, re-encrypts, and retries invisibly to the staffer. A long BAS run that straddles a token boundary depends on that refresh working cleanly.

Rate limits are the other operational reality. A month-end reconciliation sweep across 200 tenants runs into Xero's API limits fast. xero-ecosystem-integration-agent carries the rate-limit management and retry-with-backoff logic. enterprise-integration-orchestrator reports which tenants completed and which queued. No figure is relied on from a degraded channel.

What never lives in a config file — regulated figures stay in engines

The hard rule, restated: a regulated figure never hides in a config file. It lives in an engine. The constitution names the rate — SG 12%, GST 10%, tax-free threshold $18,200 — and cites the source. The computation is done by a deterministic engine whose output is identical for identical inputs. The constitution is the citation, the as-at date, and the gate policy. It is not the arithmetic.

The reason is defence. A config file can be silently edited, go stale, or be mis-read. An engine's output can be re-derived and defended to a regulator or a court. advisory-orchestrator models this exactly. It cites the 50% CGT discount under ITAA 1997 Div 115, the small-business 50% active-asset reduction under Subdiv 152-C (s 152-205), and the retirement exemption of $500,000 under s 152-320. Each value is traced to its source. The discount computation itself is engine-owned.

Constitution cites, engine computes. advisory-orchestrator returns a small-business CGT position citing ITAA 1997 Div 115 for the 50% discount and Subdiv 152-C (s 152-205) for the active-asset 50% reduction. The engine applies the discounts to the engine-computed gain. The agent reports the figure verbatim with its citations.

Figure hides in a prompt. A staffer hard-codes "GST = 9%" into an agent prompt to speed up a one-off. The output does not match the engine's 10%. The incident protocol fires. The hard-coded figure is rejected. The hard rule enforced in code.

The boundary tests live in the constitution's gate policy, not in the agent's discretion. GST coding is A unless it interprets tax law, then it is H3. An SG shortfall calculation is H3 because advising on the charge is a tax-agent service. Those are not calls the agent gets to negotiate. They are rules the constitution carries and compliance-agent enforces.

The firm manages skills the same way it manages the constitution files: in git, with a review gate, with a rollback. A new client policy skill lands as a pull request. The practice manager reviews the tool allow-list — no skill may grant a write tool without a matching gate. The skill merges and ships to every seat on the next plugin pull. A skill that carried a regulated figure would fail review, because the figure belongs in the engine module.

Hooks — firm-wide pre- and post-tool compliance gates

Hooks are where determinism lives at the tool boundary. A hook is a script that fires before or after every tool call, deterministically, with no LLM in the path. The scaffold ships three. pre_tool_use.sh enforces the gate: tenant and token present, mode defaults to dry-run, live-run requires an approval token, and a GST rule re-checks any create-invoice call. post_tool_use.sh seals the hash: timestamp, idempotency key, tool name, and arguments hash land in the audit log. on_output_validate.sh lints the output: jsonschema plus a regulatory lint that catches a mis-coded GST fraction before it leaves the agent.

Basic 3 — a pre-invoice hook blocks a GST-rule breach. invoice-processing-agent calls create-invoice for $5,500 including GST. pre_tool_use.sh recomputes GST = $500, matches the invoice line, the gate passes, post_tool_use.sh logs the hash. The post-tool seal is the other half of the gate. Every emission that passes the pre-hook is sealed by the post-hook. The hash is what audit-trail-agent chains into the immutable trail. A figure without a sealed hash did not happen, as far as the evidence pack is concerned.

# hooks/post_tool_use.sh — the seal
LOG_FILE="artifacts/audit.log"
echo "$(date -Iseconds) | ${CLDR_IDEMPOTENCY_KEY} | ${CLDR_MCP_TOOL_NAME} | \
  $(echo -n "${CLDR_MCP_TOOL_ARGS}" | sha256sum | cut -d' ' -f1)" >> "$LOG_FILE"

Basic 5 — idempotency key blocks a double-post. The same create-invoice call lands twice on a network retry. The idempotency key SHA256(invoice,INV-031,T-042) matches the first call's record. The second call is a no-op. post_tool_use.sh records the dedup. No duplicate invoice, no duplicate GST, no reconciliation break downstream.

MCP — the governed bridge to Xero, XPM, and the ATO

MCP is the Model Context Protocol — the spec that exposes tools, resources, and prompts to a host. For this firm it is the governed bridge to the systems of record. Three realities shape how the firm deploys it, and the third is the one most practitioners miss.

First, the Xero Accounting MCP server is real and production-grade. npx @xeroapi/xero-mcp-server@latest exposes roughly forty read tools and twelve write tools against the ledger, AR/AP, contacts, bank transactions, and reports. Read tools are A-class — a list-aged-receivables-by-contact call for tenant T-042 returns the AR aging and surfaces it to the partner dashboard with no gate. Write tools gate behind the approval-token hook from section 05.3.

Second, XPM sits behind its own MCP bridge over the XPM REST API, exposing jobs, WIP, timesheets, and the practice-management substrate the process map in chapter 06 reads from.

Third — and this is the determinism-first boundary that bites — AU payroll and STP are not in the public Xero MCP surface. The Xero MCP server README states that payroll-specific queries require the NZ or UK region. AU STP, PAYG withholding, and the super guarantee run through the ATO's SBR2 and PLS channels, wrapped by ato-integration-agent against the spec-only ato-connector-agent. A reader who expects a list-stp-pay-events tool in Xero MCP will not find it. The ledger facts travel by MCP. The regulated payroll lodgement travels by a separate gated connector. The determinism-first boundary — where facts end and figures begin

One sentence holds the chapter together. Skills, hooks, and MCP carry facts and rules. Only the deterministic engine originates figures. A regulated figure that lives in a skill prompt or a hook script is a defect. It belongs in lib/australian-tax.ts — the engine module the GST fraction, the SG rate, and the withholding scales import from.

The staleness risk makes this concrete. Four of the agent definition files in the catalog still carry an SG rate of 11% in their inline parameter blocks — master-orchestrator, payroll-agent, tax-orchestrator, stp-compliance-agent. That figure is FY 2024-25. For FY 2025-26 the SG rate is 12% from 1 July 2025, and only superannuation-guarantee-agent carries it correctly. If the 12% lived in a skill prompt, every seat that loaded the skill would compute SG on the wrong base. Because it lives in the engine, the agent files being stale is a documentation smell, not a computation error — the engine produces 12% regardless of what the prompt says. Chapter 08 runs the full payroll bureau on that guarantee.

# lib/australian-tax.ts — the engine module. Figures live here, nowhere else.
export const GST_RATE = 0.10;
export const SG_RATE_FY2025_26 = 0.12;   // from 1 July 2025
export const BAS_VARIANCE_TOLERANCE = 2.00;
// agent files cite these for documentation; the engine computes with these

Stale constants are the silent killer. A wrong routing target fails loudly — the host throws "agent not found." A wrong constant in a prompt fails silently — the model cites a stale rate with full confidence. Keep every regulated figure in the engine module. Re-baseline the engine every 1 July. Treat the inline parameter blocks in agent files as documentation, never as the source of a figure you rely on.

Payday Super — enacted, and the quarterly clock sunsets on 30 June 2026

Everything in section 08.4 describes the quarterly system, which is the law for FY 2025-26 as at June 2026. It sunsets days later. The Treasury Laws Amendment (Payday Superannuation) Act 2025 and the Superannuation Guarantee Charge Amendment Act 2025 are enacted: from 1 July 2026, SG is paid per payday, and each contribution must reach the employee's fund within 7 business days of the pay. The earnings base changes from ordinary time earnings to qualifying earnings (QE). The maximum contribution base becomes an annual $270,830 instead of a quarterly figure. The Small Business Superannuation Clearing House closes on 30 June 2026. And the superannuation guarantee charge is restructured with new penalty settings for the per-payday regime — the engine's SGC formula in this chapter is the FY 2025-26 one and re-baselines with everything else on 1 July.

The engine boundary does not move. The SG percentage stays at 12% — the legislated glide path is complete and no further rate step exists. What re-baselines on 1 July 2026 is the timing parameter: per-payday due dates replace the 28-days-after-quarter clock, and the real-time shortfall detector in section 08.4 becomes the primary control rather than the backstop. A bureau that already runs the detector every pay run is close to ready. One that reconciles quarterly is not. The H2 gate on any SGC matter is unchanged.

Supply classification — taxable, GSTfree, input taxed

goods-services-tax-agent owns the classification call. Three buckets govern every supply, and the engine only runs once the bucket is set. Taxable supplies carry 10 per cent GST — retail, most services, commercial rent. GST-free supplies carry 0 per cent GST but still let you claim the full input tax credit — basic food, exports, most health and education, and charitable supplies. The BAS labels map onto those buckets. G1 is total sales. G2 is exports. G3 is other GST-free. G10 is capital purchases. G11 is non-capital purchases. 1A is GST on sales. 1B is GST on purchases. The engine owns every label once the classification is set. The LLM owns the classification rationale, and that rationale is an ATO audit focus because GST-free classification is the most common error the regulator finds.

The taxable-supply test is the nine-element gate behind every classification. A supply is taxable if all nine elements hold: you make the supply for consideration, in the course of an enterprise, connected with Australia, by a registered entity, and it is not GST-free or input-taxed. The engine applies the test mechanically once the facts are in. The agent documents the facts. A failure on any single element flips the supply out of taxable.

# The taxable-supply test the engine runs (GST Act 1999 s 9-5)
def is_taxable(supply):
    return (supply.consideration and supply.in_enterprise
        and supply.connected_with_australia and supply.entity_registered
        and not supply.gst_free and not supply.input_taxed)
#   → all nine elements true → taxable → engine applies 1/11th

Margin scheme, reverse charge, and amendments

Two specialist classifications route to goods-services-tax-agent and gate at R for judgement: the margin scheme and the reverse charge. Both carry large dollar consequences and both are ATO audit magnets. The engine owns the margin arithmetic. The agent owns the eligibility determination and the documentation.

Intermediate 4 — margin scheme application. A property developer sells a townhouse for $400,000. The acquisition cost was $250,000. Under the margin scheme (Div 75), GST is computed on the margin: $400,000 minus $250,000 = $150,000 margin, times 1/11 = $13,636 GST. Under the standard method, GST would be $400,000 times 1/11 = $36,364. The saving is large. The eligibility requires eligible property, a registered supplier, and a written agreement before settlement. goods-services-tax-agent confirms eligibility and documents the basis. gst-bas-agent computes the margin. The consistency of method across prior sales is the audit focus.

# gst-bas-agent margin scheme (engine owns the margin arithmetic)
margin_gst = gst_engine.margin_scheme(sale=400000, acquisition=250000)
#   margin = 400000 - 250000 = 150000
#   gst    = 150000 / 11     = 13636.36  (engine-owned)
#   vs standard method: 400000 / 11 = 36363.64 → saving 22727.28

Reverse charge. Imported services and intangibles bought from a non-resident without an Australian establishment trigger the reverse charge. The recipient accounts for the GST instead of the supplier. The registration threshold is $75,000. The reverse charge applies when the recipient is registered (or required to be) and acquires imported services or intangibles otherwise than solely for a creditable purpose (GST Act 1999 Div 84); there is no dollar de minimis. goods-services-tax-agent determines whether the rule fires. The engine computes the GST payable. The documentation of the connection-with-Australia test is the audit focus.

Amendments. The GST amendment window is four years for most entities. bas-agent and gst-bas-agent support the amendment via the /bas-amendment command. A voluntary disclosure reduces the penalty exposure, which can run up to 75 per cent of the shortfall for intentional disregard (plus uplift in repeat cases). The amendment is drafted by the agent. The lodgement of the amendment is an H2 act. The engine computes the margin only after goods-services-tax-agent has documented eligibility on the facts. The registered human carries the lodgement and the audit risk. Never let the agent originate the method choice.

Month-end as a composed cycle, not a scramble

The scramble exists because every stage depends on the one before it and nobody owns the sequence. Bank rec blocks allocations. Allocations block AR and AP. AR and AP block the accruals. The accruals block the management pack. One late stage cascades into a late close, and the late close cascades into a late management report the client no longer trusts. The agentic rebuild fixes the root cause. orchestrator owns the sequence. The sequence is a runbook. The runbook is version-controlled. The stages run in parallel where they are independent and in sequence where they depend.

The agent-native endpoint is continuous-close. The ledger is always period-close-ready because reconciliation, coding, and accrual happen per-transaction, not at month-end. Month-end collapses to confirm-and-sign, not prepare-and-pray. The H3 gate stays. The scramble goes. The determinism-first boundary is what makes continuous-close defensible: the cleared balance, the GST total, the PAYG total, and the cash total are all engine-owned, so the close is a reconciliation of deterministic figures, not a recomputation of them.

13 The Workpaper and Audit-Trail Spine

The spine — every agent output traceable to a source

A firm that bolts agents onto a time-cost model gets speed. A firm that builds the spine gets defensibility. Those are different goals, and only one of them survives an audit. The spine exists for the second. Its purpose is that any regulated figure the firm has ever emitted can be walked back, hop by hop, to the engine that computed it and the source the engine cited, without a human hunting through email.

The spine is composed, not added on. audit-trail-agent is the substrate: an append-only, hash-chained ledger that captures every transaction, every system event, and every compliance event with a chain-of-custody and a digital signature. The determinism-first boundary holds all the way up the spine. The spine carries facts and citations, never figures. The engine originates every regulated number: the GST fraction, the cleared-balance arithmetic, the withholding table. The agents route, draft, gather citations, and chase. The single highest residual risk a firm carries is a hallucinated citation in a tax or law workpaper, because a confident wrong cite reads identically to a confident right one until the auditor opens the file. The spine is the answer to that risk.

The defensibility test the spine exists to satisfy has several limbs, and each one is an envelope field. Provenance is the citation chain back to a source chunk. Integrity is the content hash that proves the figure was not altered in transit. Currency is the as-at date that proves the cited rate was current when the workpaper sealed. Completeness is the absence of gaps in the custody chain. Segregation is the proof that author and reviewer differ. A workpaper that passes all five limbs is defensible. A workpaper that fails any one is not. The spine does not make the firm faster at producing workpapers. It makes the firm faster at producing workpapers that pass the test, because the test runs at seal time rather than at audit time.

The provenance envelope — citation, as-at, noOverride

The atom of the spine is the provenance envelope. It is a JSON object every agent emits alongside any regulated output. The mandated fields: author_agent and semver, model and residency, prompt_hash, retrieved_context[] with a version and as_at_date per chunk, tool_calls[] with result hashes, content_hash, autonomy_tag, reserved_acts[], reviewer and signature, and retention_class. Three of these are load-bearing for this chapter.

The first is citation. Every regulated claim links to a retrieved_context chunk returned by knowledge-base-agent, which reports 98.5% citation accuracy. An uncited regulated claim fails the defensibility test. The second is the as-at date. Every chunk carries valid_from and valid_to, and the envelope stamps the as_at_date the rate was current. A workpaper citing the super guarantee must carry the as-at date that 12% is the rate for FY 2025-26 (as at June 2026, per the ATO). The third is noOverride. Once an autonomous agent emits an envelope, a downstream agent cannot silently alter the cited figure or the citation. The only permitted edit is a new versioned chunk with the old one flipped to superseded. The trail is never deleted.

14 Governance: Human Gates, Segregation of Duties and PI Insurance

The H1/H2/H3 gate model at firm scale — and the liability that stays with the registered humans

A firm is not an agent stack with humans attached. It is a human control plane wrapped around an agent stack, and the control plane is where every regulated act is owned, signed, and held to account. This chapter sets out the three gates that bound the bench, the segregation-of-duties rule that stops any one agent or person from both computing and approving a figure, and the professional indemnity insurance that follows the registered human regardless of how much of the drafting moved into the agents.

The human control plane — partners, managers, reviewers

The bench does the drafting. The control plane does the deciding. Three tiers map directly to the gate model. Partners own the non-delegable and registered sign-offs, the H1 and H2 gates. Managers own the practitioner review, the H3 gate, and the segregation role of approver where the preparer is someone else. Reviewers and the bench own the second pair of eyes. master-orchestrator is the routing surface that carries work between these tiers, but it never holds a gate. It routes to the human who does.

The orchestrator's escalation criteria are explicit in its definition. Fraud, amounts in dispute over $100,000, legal proceedings, multi-jurisdictional issues, and professional-conduct concerns always escalate to a human. None of those is configurable down to an agent. The control plane is the part of the firm that cannot shrink past the law, and the law names exactly these acts.

What the human must never delegate is the short list at the centre of this chapter. Forming and signing an audit opinion. The going-concern judgement. The independence threat-significance call. Lodging a BAS or a return. Giving a personal recommendation under an AFS licence. These are the acts the gate model exists to hold, and the rest of the chapter is the mechanics of holding them without friction where friction is not owed and without exception where it is.

The control plane is also where the firm's memory lives. A partner who leaves takes tacit knowledge out the door, and in a lean firm that loss is disproportionately felt. The spine from chapter 13 is the partial answer: the sealed trail holds the reasoning the partner would have carried in their head, so the next reviewer inherits the judgement, not just the output. Pair the spine with a documented gate policy and the firm's governance survives a principal departing. Skip both and the firm's governance lives in one person's inbox.

Basic 1 — classify an activity into H1, H2, or H3. A principal reads three acts off a desk. "Lodge a BAS" is H2, because a registered tax or BAS agent signs and the act is reserved. "Sign an audit opinion" is H1, because only the registered company auditor forms and signs it. "Draft a tax-position memo" is H3, because it is judgement the firm relies on but a manager or partner reviews first. One activity, one gate, one source. The classification is the first skill a controller on this bench learns.

The H1/H2/H3 gate model applied firm-wide

The book's three-gate shorthand maps onto the reserved-acts structure the firm actually enforces. H1 is the non-delegable sign-off. The acts that never leave the responsible principal regardless of agent capability: forming and signing an audit opinion, signing an assurance conclusion, the going-concern judgement, the independence threat-significance call. The agent may prepare. The licensee performs.

H2 is the registered professional. The acts that require a registered credential to take effect: lodging a BAS or IAS or return, lodging an STP Phase 2 pay event, representing a client before the Commissioner under s 90-5(b) of the TASA 2009, signing a general-purpose financial statement or compilation, an SMR lodgement. The agent assembles and stops. The registered human signs and lodges.

H3 is the practitioner review. The judgement and reliance gate: drafting adjusting journals, a tax-position memo at a reasonably-arguable position above 50% confidence, a going-concern analysis draft, a CGT classification. The agent drafts. A manager or partner reviews and approves before the output can be relied on or cross the firm boundary.

Segregation of duties as the agent-enforced control

Segregation of duties is the firm's oldest control, now agent-enforced rather than policy-hoped-for. The rule is one sentence. The agent or person who computes a regulated figure never approves it. Mechanically, audit-trail-agent stamps every envelope with author_agent and reviewer. compliance-agent hash-compares the two before any gate releases. Author is not reviewer. The drafter of a tax-position memo cannot be its H3 reviewer. Compute is not approve. Governance is not a one-off gate. It is a maintained posture. Three operational disciplines hold the posture over time. From time-cost to value pricing — what changes and what doesn't

Value pricing is the commercial thesis of this chapter. Fix the definitions on first use. Utilisation is billable hours divided by available hours. Realisation is invoiced divided by standard. A firm whose realised rate drifts below 85% is discounting its outcomes back to time-cost without admitting it.

What changes is what the fee attaches to. The fee attaches to the outcome and to the gate depth. A lodged BAS, a clean ledger, a continuous-close subscription, a cited tax-defence memo. The agent-native grammar turns projects into subscriptions. Real-time bookkeeping-as-a-service runs at an illustrative $80 to $300 per client per week. Continuous compliance monitoring runs at $250 to $2,000 per entity per month. The anomaly-of-the-week advisory retainer runs at $750 to $2,500 per month. Treat these as indicative Sydney and Melbourne 2026 bands, not prescribed prices. Run your own pricing work off the reporting agent's profitability feedback loop.

What does not change is the perimeter. The professional-duty perimeter under APES 110, APES 315, and the TPB Code survives the transition intact. The determinism-first rule holds regardless of how the firm prices: the engine owns every regulated number. The conduct floor holds: the lean firm owes the same competence, reasonable care, and independence duties as a 200-person firm. Value pricing changes the invoice. It does not change the duty.

One practical point on the transition. A firm does not move from time-cost to value pricing in a single quarter, and the agents make the ramp safer. The bench makes the comparison continuous, so the firm re-prices on evidence rather than on a guess at what the market will bear.
